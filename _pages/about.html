Research

"I Feel Myself So Small!": Designing and Evaluating VR Awe Experiences Based on Theories Related to Sublime

  ISMAR 2024 (in press)

He, Z., Fan, M., Guo, X., Zhao, Y., Wang, Y.

Abstract: Research suggests the potential of employing VR to elicit awe experiences, thereby promoting well-being. Building upon theories related to the sublime and embodiment, we designed three VR scenes to evaluate the effectiveness of sublime and embodied design elements in invoking awe experiences. We conducted a within-subject study involving 28 young adults who experienced the three VR designs. Results demonstrated that the VR design with sublime elements significantly elicited more intense awe experiences compared to the one without, while adding embodied elements did not enhance the intensity of awe. Qualitative interviews revealed critical design elements (e.g., the obscure event should be reasonable) and their underlying mechanisms (e.g., leading to feelings of enlightenment) in invoking awe experiences. We further discuss considerations and implications for the design of effective awe-inspiring VR applications.

"I Recall the Past": Exploring How People Collaborate with Generative AI to Create Cultural Heritage Narratives

  CSCW 2025 (accepted)

He, Z., Su, J., Chen, L., Wang, T., LC, R.

Visitors to cultural heritage sites often encounter official information, while local people’s unofficial stories remain invisible. To explore generative AI’s potential in assisting individuals to access local narratives, we conducted a workshop with 20 participants, asking them to use Stable Diffusion to create images of familiar cultural heritage sites, as well as images of unfamiliar ones for comparison. The results revealed three narrative strategies and highlighted generative AI’s strengths in illuminating, amplifying, and reinterpreting personal narratives. However, the AI showed limitations in meeting detailed requirements, portraying cultural features, and avoiding bias, which were particularly pronounced with unfamiliar sites due to participants’ lack of local knowledge. To address these challenges, we recommend providing detailed explanations, prompt engineering, and fine-tuning AI models to reduce uncertainties, using objective references to mitigate inaccuracies from participants’ inability to recognize errors or misconceptions, and curating datasets to train AI models capable of accurately portraying cultural features.

Designing VR Awe-Inspiring Environment for Children

  (in preparation)

He, Z., Fan, M., Wang, Y., Guo, X., Zhao, Y.

Awe has beem found to effectively enhance resilience by fostering a sense of meaning, evoking gratitude, and promoting social connectedness, which can help Chinese children manage the stress and anxiety that arise from a highly competitive learning environment. Following the positive outcomes of our VR awe-inspiring experience research with young adults, we aimed to explore whether the same VR design could effectively elicit awe in children and how they would perceive the virtual experience. We conducted an experiment with 42 children aged 11-12. Preliminary results indicate that the VR awe-inspiring scene successfully induced awe compared to the VR control scene. Additionally, we observed some common responses across all VR experiences. For instance, participants tended to seek out familiar virtual elements to establish an initial sense of safety. These findings could inform the design of VR experiences specifically tailored for inducing positive emotions in children.

Exploring Broadly Applicable Awe-Inspiring Design Elements and its effectivenss in VR and video medium

  (in preparation)

He, Z., Xie, Z., Wang, Z., Gao, Z.

In the first project, I noticed a phenomenon that participants’ awe intensity diminish when they were repeatedly exposed to the same scene in the second week. This led me to initiated a follow-up research project in collaboration with Dr. Gao, aiming to develop a design principle applicable to creating different kinds of VR awe experience that users can switch between to maintain the quality of awe. Additionally, some participants mentioned that while they had seen many videos of huge waves or a vast moon, witnessing these phenomena in VR felt entirely different. This raised my curiosity about what role VR plays in inducing awe compared to other media. I also wondered whether the effectiveness of media varies across different kinds of awe experiences.
To develop design principle, I conducted a systematic literature review and inductively summarized general design elements of awe stimuli from existing research. I systematically reviewed papers on awe induction from 2003 to 2023 from sources like ACM, IEEE, and APA PsycINFO, analyzing common elements across different forms of awe experiences, such as newspapers, pictures, and videos. Based on these elements we designed five different kinds of awe experiences in both VR and video formats for the experiment, including scene of mountain views, indoor fires, light speed, water freezing, and cultural relic. Primary analysis of the data showed that two relatively familiar awe-inspiring scenes for participants were ineffective at inducing awe in the video medium but became effective in VR. This can attribute to the sense of presence, which made participants felt as though they were truly in the situation, rather than merely being bystanders. I also found that the water freezing scene, the most surreal one, induced significantly higher presence than the control scene in the video condition, suggesting that intense, “impossible” experiences can enhance the sense of presence within the same medium. These findings not only confirm VR’s outstanding capacity to induce high-quality awe compared to video, but also reveal the interaction between contents and media in influencing people’s perception.
